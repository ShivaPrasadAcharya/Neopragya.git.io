B2

Basic Structure Doctrine

Keshavananda Bharati Case:

  • Total Days: Longest marathon 68 days hearing.
  • Bench: Largest bench in the history of Supreme Court of India (13 Justices) led by CJ Sikri and the judgement was delivered on the last working day of CJ Sikri. Upon his retirement the tradition of appointing the senior most judge as Chief Justice was abandoned and Justice Ray, who was fourth in the line of seniority, superseded other 3 justices and all three of them later resigned.
  • Hearing Days: This case was heard for 6 months from October 31, 1972- March 23, 1973. ( Monday, Wednesday and Thursday)
  • Lawyer: Nani Palkhiwala was the leading lawyer teamed with CK Daphtary, MC Chagla and was assisted by Soli Soarabjee, Anil Divan DM Popat, and Fali Nariman and others. For Keshavananda Bharati.
  • Funding: Funding of the litigation was done by Coal, Mining and Sugar Company.
  • Residence: Lawyers were kept at Oberoi Hotel during hearing days.
  • Counter Team: Counter team was led by HM Seervai ( State of Kerala) and Niren De ( AG UOI).
  • Time Division: Palkhiwala and his. team argued for 31 days. Palkhiwala rejoined pleaded for 2 days. HM Seervai argued for 21 days Niren Dde (AG of India) and team argued for 12 days.
  • Pages: Longest judgement of 703 pages.
  • Judgement: Golakhnath was overruled by 9 of the 13 judges ( there was division of justices in various points to by 7:6). Formulation of Basic Structure Doctrine
  • Basic Structure Doctrine: Ratio Power of the Parliament to amend the Constitution would not include the power to alter its basic structure or essential features- Basic Structure Doctrine.
  • Consequences: By this case Supreme Court strengthen the power of judicial review and ipso facto, placed a limitation on the parliament's power to amend the constitution. This is the case from which the reputation and recognition of the constitutional lawyers was heighten. That time Indira Gandhi was the Prime Minister of India with stunning victory, gathering almost 350 seats out of the total strength of 540 and has pressing interest of Government in this case.
  • Experience:Justice Jagmohan (One among 13) said, “ I got the impression from the first day of hearing that minds of justices were closed and views were determined."

Hart-Fuller Debate

  • Issue of Debate: Debate was based on post world war decision of the German Court in 1950.
  • Hart's View: The existence of law is one thing and its merit and demerits another. Law is law whether it is moral or immoral. What is morally wrong can still be legally right. So, how immoral the Nazi's laws might be it must be obeyed.
  • Fuller's View: Laws in themselves should be moral. Immoral laws (laws without inner morality) are not laws at all. It is not necessary to obey the immoral laws of Nazi government.

Hart-Delvin Debate

  • Issue of Debate: Debate was based on the Wolfeden Report 1957.
  • Hart's View:He supported the philosophy of the report and agreed that breaching of morality in private (isolation) do not affect the integrity of society. Therefore, homosexual activity in private should not be criminalized.
  • Delvin's View: He opposed the philosophy of report. Morality is morality. It cannot be public or private. Society has right to pass and enforce law on moral matters. Therefore, homosexual activities in private should also be the matter of criminalization.

Judicial Activism

Characteristics of Judicial Activism

Judicial activism is a concept in which judges interpret the law in a way that goes beyond the traditional role of the judiciary as a neutral and passive arbiter of disputes. Here are some key characteristics of judicial activism:

  • Expansive Interpretation of the Law: Judicial activism involves an expansive interpretation of the law in order to achieve certain policy objectives. This means that judges may read the law in a broader and more flexible way in order to achieve a desired outcome
  • Active Role of the Judiciary: Judicial activism also involves a more active role for the judiciary in shaping public policy. This can include the use of the courts to advance social, economic, or political change
  • Protection of Minority Rights: Judicial activism is often associated with the protection of minority rights. This can include decisions that strike down laws that discriminate against certain groups or that infringe upon individual rights
  • Challenge to Legislative Supremacy: Judicial activism can also involve a challenge to the principle of legislative supremacy. This means that judges may strike down laws or interpret them in a way that goes against the will of the legislature
  • Use of Judicial Review: Judicial activism often involves the use of judicial review, which is the power of the judiciary to review and potentially invalidate actions taken by the executive or legislative branches of government
